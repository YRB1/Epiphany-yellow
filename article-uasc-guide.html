Supporting Unaccompanied Asylum-Seeking Children: A Carer's Guide

Every unaccompanied asylum-seeking child who arrives in the UK has made a journey most adults could not imagine. They have survived things no child should face. As a foster carer, you do not need to fix what happened. What you can do is provide safety, stability, and the chance to begin again.

Who are unaccompanied asylum-seeking children?

Unaccompanied asylum-seeking children (UASC) are young people under 18 who have arrived in the UK without a parent or legal guardian and who are seeking protection here. They come from countries including Afghanistan, Eritrea, Sudan, Syria, Albania, Iran, and Vietnam, among others.

They are looked-after children in the eyes of the law. Local authorities have a legal duty to accommodate and care for them. Many are placed with independent fostering agencies like Epiphany because of our specialist expertise in meeting their complex needs.

UASC are not a homogeneous group. They vary enormously in age, language, culture, religion, the nature of their journeys, and the traumas they have experienced. The one thing they share is that they are children far from home, alone, and in need of care.

Legal status explained

Understanding a young person's legal status helps you support them effectively. Most UASC will be in one of these situations:

  • Awaiting initial asylum decision. The young person has claimed asylum and is waiting for the Home Office to make a decision. This can take months or years and is a major source of anxiety.
  • Granted refugee status or humanitarian protection. The young person has been recognised as a refugee or granted leave to remain. This provides greater stability but may have a time limit.
  • Granted UASC leave. Some young people receive limited leave to remain until they turn 17.5 or for 30 months, whichever is longer. This must then be reviewed.
  • Appeal pending. A young person whose claim has been refused may be appealing. This is a period of acute anxiety.
Note for carers

You are not responsible for managing a young person's immigration case. They will have an immigration solicitor. Your role is to support their wellbeing. Epiphany's UASC team will keep you informed of key developments.

Understanding trauma in UASC

UASC have often experienced multiple traumatic events: armed conflict, family separation or bereavement, persecution, dangerous journeys, exploitation, and violence. Trauma affects the brain and body. Young people may show hypervigilance, sleep disturbances, flashbacks, emotional numbness, or intense reactions to triggers that seem minor to an outside observer.

Understanding trauma changes how you interpret behaviour. A young person who refuses food is not being difficult. A teenager who will not go upstairs alone is not attention-seeking. These are trauma responses, and they require patience and consistency rather than consequences.

The first days at home

The first few days with a UASC child can feel overwhelming for both of you. The young person may not speak English, may be exhausted, may be in shock, and may be deeply distrustful of adults. This is entirely normal.

Your priority is simple: help them feel physically safe. A warm bed, regular food, warmth, and freedom from immediate threat are what the nervous system needs before any other kind of support can land. Do not rush to fill silences with questions. Sit with them. Watch a film. Make meals together. Let them set the pace.

Practical support

  • Help registering with a GP and dentist
  • Supporting access to ESOL (English for Speakers of Other Languages) classes
  • Assisting with school enrolment or college applications
  • Accompanying them to appointments with solicitors, social workers, and healthcare providers
  • Helping them obtain a bank account and learn to navigate basic UK systems
  • Supporting them to stay connected with their community, faith, and culture

Navigating language barriers

Not speaking the same language as the young person in your care can feel like a significant obstacle, but it does not have to be. Many carers of UASC do not share their language. What matters more is warmth, consistency, and non-verbal communication.

Professional interpreters should always be used for important conversations: legal appointments, medical consultations, placement meetings, and LAC reviews. Never ask the young person to interpret for themselves. In day-to-day life, translation apps can help, but human connection does not require words.

Managing uncertainty and anxiety

For UASC young people, uncertainty about their immigration status is a constant source of anxiety. Waiting for a decision can stretch on for years. You cannot resolve this uncertainty for them. But you can help them stay grounded in the present through routine. Regular meals, bedtimes, school, and activities create a structure that the nervous system can predict, which is deeply regulating for young people who have lived through chaos.

"When he first arrived, he barely spoke. Three months later, he was helping me cook his mother's recipes and teaching me words in Pashto. The trust takes time. But it comes." Epiphany Foster Carer, West Midlands
