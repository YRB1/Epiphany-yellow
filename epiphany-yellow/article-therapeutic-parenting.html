Therapeutic Parenting: What It Means and Why It Works

Therapeutic parenting is not a therapy. You do not need a clinical qualification to practise it. It is a way of being with children who have experienced trauma: one that prioritises connection over correction, understanding over punishment, and relationship over rules.

What is therapeutic parenting?

Therapeutic parenting is an evidence-based approach to caring for children who have experienced developmental trauma, neglect, or abuse. It draws on attachment theory, neuroscience, and child development research to help carers understand why children in care behave as they do, and what they actually need from the adults around them.

The central insight is this: children who have grown up in unsafe or unpredictable environments have brains that have adapted to survive those environments. Their behaviour, however challenging it appears, is rooted in survival strategies that once made sense. The role of the therapeutic parent is to create enough safety, consistency, and connection that the child's nervous system can begin to settle, and new patterns of relating can form.

Why conventional parenting often falls short

Most of us were raised with an approach to parenting that assumes children are capable of reflecting on their behaviour, connecting actions to consequences, and responding to reward and sanction. For children who have experienced trauma, this often does not work. Trauma affects the prefrontal cortex, the part of the brain responsible for reasoning, planning, and impulse control. When a child is in a stress response, this part of the brain goes offline. No amount of reasoning or consequence-setting will get through, because the child is literally not in a state where their brain can process it.

This is not about the child being "bad" or "manipulative." It is about neurobiology. And understanding that changes everything.

The PACE model

One of the most widely used frameworks in therapeutic parenting is the PACE model, developed by clinical psychologist Dr Dan Hughes. PACE stands for Playfulness, Acceptance, Curiosity, and Empathy.

Playfulness

Playfulness means approaching interactions with a lightness that invites connection. Humour, warmth, and enjoyment of the child's company signal safety. They communicate: "I like you. You are not a problem to be managed."

Acceptance

Acceptance means accepting the child's inner world: their feelings, their history, their needs. It does not mean accepting harmful behaviour. "I understand why you are angry. It is not okay to hit, but the anger makes sense." This separates the child's worth from their behaviour, which is essential for a young person who may have been told repeatedly that they are the problem.

Curiosity

Curiosity means being genuinely interested in the child's inner experience. "I wonder what was happening for you when that started?" This approach helps children develop their own capacity to reflect on their feelings and behaviour, something many children in care have never been supported to do.

Empathy

Empathy means really trying to understand what the world feels like from inside the child's experience. Empathy does not fix pain, but it makes it less lonely.

Putting it into practice

  • Regulate yourself first. You cannot help a child regulate their nervous system if yours is dysregulated. Notice your own state before responding.
  • Narrate rather than interrogate. Instead of "Why did you do that?" try "I noticed you seemed really upset when your friend left. I wonder if that felt a bit like being left before?"
  • Repair after rupture. There will be moments when you get it wrong. Coming back and saying "I am sorry I snapped at you. That wasn't okay" models exactly the kind of relationship the child needs to experience.
  • Prioritise connection over correction. When a child is dysregulated, connection comes first. The behaviour conversation can wait until both of you are calm.
  • Create rhythm and routine. Predictable routines are regulating. Mealtimes, bedtimes, weekend rituals help traumatised children feel safe because they can predict what comes next.
"The shift for me was stopping asking why he was doing something, and starting to wonder what he was feeling. Everything changed after that." Epiphany Foster Carer, Leeds

Carer self-care and support

Therapeutic parenting is demanding work. Caring for children who have experienced significant trauma means absorbing a great deal of pain, fear, and anger. Compassion fatigue is real and needs to be taken seriously.

Epiphany takes carer wellbeing as seriously as child wellbeing. All our carers have access to regular supervision, peer support groups, and respite care arrangements. Looking after yourself is not a luxury. It is an essential part of looking after the children in your care.
