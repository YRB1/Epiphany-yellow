Understanding Developmental Trauma and Attachment in Children in Care

To care well for children who have experienced trauma, you first need to understand what trauma actually is and what it does to a developing child. This is not theoretical knowledge. It changes how you see behaviour, how you respond, and ultimately how much difference you can make.

What is developmental trauma?

Developmental trauma refers to the impact of repeated, prolonged adverse experiences during childhood, particularly experiences that occur within the care relationship itself. This includes abuse, neglect, domestic violence, parental mental illness, substance misuse, and severe poverty.

What makes developmental trauma distinct from single-incident trauma is that it occurs precisely in the relationships where children are supposed to find safety. When the person who is meant to protect you is also the source of harm, or when the primary caregiver is simply absent or unable to respond to your needs, this creates a particular kind of damage that affects the child's entire developmental trajectory.

It affects how their brain develops. How they regulate their emotions. How they form relationships. How they see themselves. And, crucially, how they behave.

How trauma affects the developing brain

The human brain develops in a use-dependent way: neural pathways that are used frequently become stronger, while those that are not used wither. A child who grows up in an environment of threat, unpredictability, or emotional deprivation develops a brain that is exquisitely attuned to danger and survival, but less well-developed in areas that support reasoning, empathy, and self-regulation.

The amygdala, which processes threat responses, becomes hypersensitive. The prefrontal cortex, which handles planning, impulse control, and rational thought, develops more slowly or less fully. The result is a child who is chronically on alert, who reacts to perceived threats with fight, flight, or freeze responses that can look to outsiders like aggression, defiance, or "shutting down."

This is not a moral failing. It is neurological adaptation. And it is reversible, given the right experiences over time.

Attachment theory: the basics

Attachment theory, developed by John Bowlby and later extended by Mary Ainsworth, describes the deep emotional bond that forms between an infant and their primary caregiver. This bond is not optional: it is biologically driven, because a dependent infant literally cannot survive without care.

When a caregiver is consistently available, responsive, and attuned to a child's needs, the child develops what is called a secure attachment. They learn that the world is basically safe, that other people can be trusted, that they are worthy of care. This secure base allows them to explore, to learn, to form relationships, and to manage distress.

When a caregiver is inconsistent, frightening, absent, or abusive, the child develops one of several forms of insecure or disorganised attachment. These early attachment patterns shape relationships throughout life.

Attachment patterns you may see in children in care

Anxious / ambivalent attachment

Children with anxious attachment are hypervigilant to signs of abandonment. They may be clingy, demanding, or prone to intense emotional reactions when separated from their carer, even briefly. They may struggle to be soothed and may seem inconsolable. This pattern develops when caregiving has been inconsistent: sometimes available, sometimes not.

Avoidant attachment

Children with avoidant attachment have learned that expressing need does not result in care, or results in rejection or punishment. They appear self-sufficient, emotionally flat, or detached. They may resist comfort, avoid eye contact, or seem unaffected by things that should distress them. This is not resilience. It is suppression.

Disorganised attachment

Disorganised attachment develops when the caregiver is the source of both comfort and fear. The child is caught in an unresolvable paradox: to seek safety, they must approach the very person who frightens them. This results in contradictory, chaotic, or confused behaviour. Disorganised attachment is associated with the most significant long-term difficulties and is common in children who have experienced abuse within the care relationship.

Understanding what behaviour is communicating

When you understand trauma and attachment, behaviour starts to make different sense. The child who steals food is not greedy. They are recreating a survival response to deprivation. The teenager who pushes you away every time you get close is testing whether you will stay, because everyone else has left. The child who explodes over something minor has a nervous system calibrated for catastrophe and cannot tell the difference between a small inconvenience and a genuine threat.

This reframing does not mean accepting harmful behaviour. It means responding to it differently. Instead of "what is wrong with you?" the question becomes "what has happened to you, and what are you trying to tell me?"

"I used to think she was deliberately trying to wind me up. Once I understood that her brain was stuck in survival mode, I stopped taking it personally. That changed everything for both of us." Epiphany Foster Carer, London

The path to healing

The good news is that the brain retains plasticity throughout childhood and into adulthood. Given the right relational experiences, children can and do form new neural pathways. Secure attachment with a consistent, attuned caregiver, whether a foster carer, adoptive parent, or other stable adult figure, is the most powerful mechanism for this healing.

It does not happen quickly. It does not happen without difficulty. There will be periods when the child tests the relationship to its limits, unconsciously checking whether you will do what every other adult has done and disappear, reject, or hurt them. Staying steady through these moments, repairing after ruptures, and continuing to offer warmth and consistency is the work. And it is extraordinary work.
