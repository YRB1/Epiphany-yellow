What Is Cultural Identity and Why Does It Matter in Foster Care?

A child's cultural identity is not a box to tick on a placement form. It is the foundation of who they are, how they see themselves, and how they relate to the world around them. For children in foster care, protecting and nurturing that identity is one of the most important things a carer can do.

What is cultural identity?

Cultural identity refers to the sense of belonging a person has to a particular group based on shared ethnicity, nationality, language, religion, food, customs, music, and history. It shapes how we understand ourselves and how we connect with others.

For children, this identity begins forming from birth. Long before a child can articulate who they are, they absorb cultural cues from the people around them: the languages they hear, the food they eat, the celebrations they take part in, the stories they are told. These experiences become woven into their sense of self.

When a child comes into care, this process can be disrupted or even reversed. Placed with carers from a different background, in a different community, sometimes in a different part of the country, the cultural reference points that formed their earliest understanding of the world can disappear overnight.

Why cultural identity matters in foster care

The importance of maintaining a child's cultural identity in foster care is not just a matter of good practice guidelines. It is rooted in research, in child development science, and in the lived experiences of thousands of young people who have grown up in the care system.

Studies consistently show that children who maintain a strong connection to their cultural heritage have better emotional wellbeing, higher self-esteem, and more positive outcomes in education and adult life. They are better equipped to navigate the challenges of adolescence, form healthy relationships, and manage the specific pressures that come with being a looked-after child.

In contrast, children who experience cultural disconnection, particularly Black and mixed-heritage children placed with white carers who lack cultural awareness, are at significantly higher risk of identity confusion, low self-esteem, mental health difficulties, and a sense of not belonging anywhere.

"Children need to know their story. Where they come from, what makes them who they are. When we ignore that, we are not just ignoring a detail. We are asking a child to become someone else." Dr. Amara Osei-Bonsu, Epiphany Foster Care

The impact of cultural identity loss

The effects of cultural disconnection can be subtle at first. A child who stops speaking their home language. A teenager who feels embarrassed about their heritage. A young person who cannot answer the question "where are you from?" without anxiety.

Over time, these experiences accumulate. Research on transracial adoption and fostering has documented a pattern sometimes called "identity foreclosure," where young people from minority ethnic backgrounds placed in predominantly white environments begin to reject their own heritage in order to fit in, only to struggle profoundly with identity in later life.

This is not inevitable. With the right support, the right training, and the right match, foster carers can actively build and reinforce a child's cultural identity, even across cultural differences. But it requires awareness, commitment, and practical action.

Practical steps for carers

Supporting a child's cultural identity does not require you to share their background. What it requires is curiosity, respect, and a genuine commitment to learning. Here are some concrete ways carers can make a difference:

  • Learn about the child's culture. Read, ask questions, watch films, try food. Show genuine interest, not performance.
  • Connect the child with their community. Find local cultural organisations, community groups, places of worship, or events that reflect the child's heritage.
  • Celebrate cultural milestones. Whether that is Eid, Diwali, Hanukkah, or a national day from the child's country of origin, mark these occasions with the same enthusiasm you would give to Christmas or Easter.
  • Use culturally appropriate hair and skin care products. This is particularly important for Black children. Consult with cultural advisors or the child's family where possible.
  • Display images that reflect diversity. Ensure the child sees themselves represented in books, artwork, and media in your home.
  • Talk openly about race and identity. Do not treat these as uncomfortable topics. Children who can discuss their identity openly with their carers develop more secure and confident self-concepts.
  • Support contact with birth family where it is safe to do so. Birth family are often the most important source of cultural connection for children in care.

Language and faith

Two particular aspects of cultural identity deserve special attention: language and faith.

For children who speak another language at home, maintaining that language is critical. A child who loses their home language loses a direct connection to their family, their heritage, and often a significant part of their cognitive identity. Carers should actively encourage home languages, seek out bilingual books and media, and never treat a child's home language as a problem to be solved.

Faith is equally important. For many children and young people, religious practice is not simply a ritual: it is a community, a set of values, a source of comfort, and a connection to ancestors and family. Carers should ensure that children are supported to observe their faith, attend their place of worship, and mark religious occasions in ways that are meaningful to them.

How Epiphany Foster Care supports this

At Epiphany Foster Care, cultural identity is not an afterthought. It is built into everything we do, from how we recruit and train carers, to how we make placement decisions, to the ongoing support we provide.

All Epiphany carers complete our specialist cultural competence training before taking on any placement. We work hard to match children with carers who share their cultural background, language, or faith where possible. Where this is not possible, we ensure carers have the knowledge, tools, and ongoing support to provide culturally affirming care across difference.

Our Supervising Social Workers actively monitor cultural identity support as part of every placement review. We ask the children in our care whether they feel their identity is being respected. We take their answers seriously.

Because a child's sense of who they are is not something that can wait until they are settled. It needs to be nurtured from day one.
